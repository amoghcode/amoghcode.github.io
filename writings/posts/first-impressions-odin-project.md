# Why I'm Learning in Public

There's a version of learning that happens quietly — you read, you practice, you get better in private, and one day you emerge competent. Nobody saw the messy middle. It's comfortable. It's also how I've always done it.

I'm trying something different now. I'm writing about what I'm learning as I learn it — before I've figured it out, before I have anything impressive to show. This essay is part of that experiment.

## The case for doing it anyway

The obvious objection is: why would anyone want to read the notes of someone who doesn't know what they're doing yet? I've thought about this. I think the answer is that the people who most need to read something are often at the same stage as the person writing it — not the expert looking back from the summit, but someone one step ahead on the same path.

There's also something that happens when you write about what you're learning. You find out quickly what you actually understand and what you only think you understand. Writing is a test. If you can't explain it clearly, you don't know it yet.

> The act of writing forces you to have an opinion about your own understanding.

## What I'm actually afraid of

Being wrong in public. Publishing something and having someone point out that I've misunderstood something basic. I think this fear is worth sitting with, because it reveals something: I care more about appearing competent than about actually becoming competent. Those two things are often in tension.

So this website is, among other things, a small attempt to care less about the first thing and more about the second.
